\noindent\textbf{Functional Variation and Euler-Lagrange Equations.}

The total action is $S[\psi] = S_{\text{kinetic}} + H_{\text{matter}}[\psi]$, where:
\[
S_{\text{kinetic}} := \int_{X \times \mathbb{R}_+} \frac{1}{2}\dot{\psi} \cdot \dot{\psi}^* dt\, d\mu(x),
\]
with $\dot{\psi} = \partial_t \psi$, and $H_{\text{matter}}$ is the Hamiltonian density (Definition \ref{def:operatorHierarchy}).

The functional variation with respect to $\bar{\psi}$ (treating $\psi$ and $\bar{\psi}$ as independent) is:
\[
\frac{\delta S}{\delta \bar{\psi}} = \frac{\delta}{\delta \bar{\psi}} \int \frac{1}{2}\partial_t \psi \partial_t \bar{\psi} + H_{\text{matter}} dt\, d\mu.
\]

The kinetic term varies as:
\[
\frac{\delta}{\delta \bar{\psi}} \int \partial_t \psi \partial_t \bar{\psi} dt\, d\mu = \partial_t^2 \psi.
\]

For the matter Hamiltonian $H_{\text{matter}}[\psi] = \int |\nabla \psi|^2 + U(|\psi|^2) d\mu$:
\[
\frac{\delta H_{\text{matter}}}{\delta \bar{\psi}} = -\frac{1}{2}\Delta_g \psi + \frac{\partial U}{\partial |\psi|^2} \cdot 2|\psi|^2 \frac{\partial |\psi|^2}{\partial \bar{\psi}}.
\]
Since $\frac{\partial |\psi|^2}{\partial \bar{\psi}} = \psi$ (complex calculus), the result is:
\[
\frac{\delta H_{\text{matter}}}{\delta \bar{\psi}} = -\frac{1}{2}\Delta_g \psi + 2U'(|\psi|^2) \psi.
\]

\noindent\textbf{Nonlinear Wave Equation.}

The Euler-Lagrange equation is:
\[
\frac{\delta S}{\delta \bar{\psi}} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \partial_t^2 \psi + \frac{1}{2}\Delta_g \psi - 2U'(|\psi|^2) \psi = 0.
\]
Rearranging:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\partial_t^2 \psi + \frac{\delta H_{\text{matter}}}{\delta \bar{\psi}} = 0},
\end{equation}
which is the stated nonlinear wave equation. The nonlinearity comes from the derivative $U'(|\psi|^2)$ in the matter Hamiltonian.

\noindent\textbf{Hamiltonian Structure.}

This equation preserves energy via Noether's theorem. The canonical energy-momentum tensor $T^{\mu\nu}$ gives:
\[
\frac{dE}{dt} = 0, \quad E = \int \frac{1}{2}|\partial_t \psi|^2 + |\nabla \psi|^2 + U(|\psi|^2) d\mu.
\]
The equation is Hamiltonian in the sense that $\partial_t \psi = \frac{\delta H}{\delta \bar{\psi}}$, where the Hamiltonian is $H = \int |\nabla \psi|^2 + U(|\psi|^2) d\mu$ (spatial part of the action), confirming that this is the correct field equation from the variational principle.
